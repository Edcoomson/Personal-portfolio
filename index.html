About

Grounded in the field,
driven by data

I am a geological engineer whose practice sits at the intersection of field geology, geospatial intelligence, and environmental data science. I have worked across alluvial gold operations, bauxite resource verification, onshore hydrocarbon mapping, and national geological survey programmes — always with one question driving the work: what does the data actually tell us about how this land behaves?

That question crystallised during my BSc thesis fieldwork. Standing at the edge of a Kumasi wetland at first light, extracting sediment cores and watching the colour change from grey to rust at exactly 14cm depth, I understood that the answer was written in the ground itself — it just needed the right tools to read it. That moment set the direction for everything since.

Three years of professional practice have sharpened that conviction. At Hawk Mining, a DEM analysis I ran in a single afternoon identified a floodplain encroachment that changed the trajectory of a resource block. At the Ghana Geological Survey Authority, a PostgreSQL database I architected eliminated manual custody gaps across 1,000+ samples. These are not big projects by global standards — but they are exactly the kind of data infrastructure that West Africa's extractive sector needs more of.

I am now pursuing a higher academic degree to build the theoretical and methodological depth that field work alone cannot provide. My mission: to build the data infrastructure that turns environmental risks into sustainable, evidence-based decisions across West Africa.

My path into geoscience began not in a lecture hall but at the edge of a wetland in Kumasi, where I first recognised that the ground beneath my feet held a record of every chemical decision the surrounding landscape had ever made. My undergraduate thesis — Geochemical Assessment of Trace Metals in Wetland Ecosystems — taught me that understanding Earth systems requires integrating chemistry, hydrology, and spatial reasoning simultaneously. It also taught me that the questions worth asking are rarely answered by a single discipline.

Three years of professional practice have sharpened that conviction. Working across alluvial gold operations, bauxite resource verification, and government geological survey programmes, I have seen repeatedly how the absence of robust, data-driven environmental models leads to reactive rather than preventive decision-making. Ghana's extractive sector operates at the intersection of significant resource wealth and genuine environmental fragility — and the tools to navigate that intersection thoughtfully are still being built.

I am pursuing a higher academic degree because I want to be among those building those tools. Specifically, I am drawn to research at the boundary of computational hydrology, geospatial remote sensing, and environmental geochemistry — using machine learning and GIS-driven modelling to quantify how mining-affected catchments exchange water, sediment, and dissolved constituents across space and time. The practical grounding I have developed in the field, combined with my programming and database skills, positions me to contribute meaningfully to this work from the first day of a research programme.

Three specific questions structure my current thinking. How can machine-learning-enhanced DEMs predict sediment transport thresholds in artisanal gold mining catchments before regulatory intervention is required? What spatial resolution is necessary for groundwater–surface water exchange models to meaningfully inform rehabilitation planning in Ghana's alluvial mining belts? And: can geochemical baselines derived from legacy survey data be reliably extended using transfer learning, reducing the cost of new environmental assessments by 20–30%? These are tractable, field-grounded questions with direct policy relevance — and they are the ones I intend to pursue.

Ultimately, I want the research I produce to be legible and useful to the communities and institutions in West Africa that are making land, water, and resource decisions right now — not only to the international journals that archive it.

2022
BSc Thesis
Geochemical Assessment of Trace Metals in Wetland Ecosystems
Coomson, E. D.
Department of Geological Engineering, Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology (KNUST), Kumasi, Ghana. Submitted in partial fulfilment of the requirements for the degree of BSc Geological Engineering.
This study investigated the spatial distribution and geochemical behaviour of selected trace metals — including Fe, Mn, Cu, Zn, Pb, and Cd — in wetland sediments and surface waters within the Kumasi metropolitan area. Trace metal concentrations were assessed against regulatory thresholds and interpreted in the context of anthropogenic loading and natural geochemical background. Results revealed elevated concentrations of Fe and Mn consistent with reducing sediment conditions, and localised Pb and Zn enrichment indicative of diffuse urban and industrial inputs. The study contributes baseline geochemical data to inform wetland management and environmental monitoring in peri-urban Ghana.

Research Agenda

Where the work is heading

BSc Thesis

"Geochemical Assessment of Trace Metals in Wetland Ecosystems"

This undergraduate investigation into heavy-metal mobility and bioavailability in Ghanaian wetlands planted the seed for my broader research interest: how surface water and groundwater systems exchange mass and energy — and how anthropogenic activity, including artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM), alters those fluxes at landscape scale. The thesis informs three prospective lines of inquiry I intend to pursue at postgraduate level.

01
MSc / PhD Direction

Data-Driven Modelling of Surface Water–Groundwater Exchange in Mining-Affected Catchments

Integrating field hydrochemistry, DEM-derived catchment delineation, and machine-learning regression to quantify groundwater contribution to stream baseflow in areas affected by alluvial gold extraction — building on my real-time GIS dashboarding work at Hawk Mining.

02
MSc / PhD Direction

Sediment Transport Dynamics and Geomorphic Change in Disturbed Fluvial Systems

Using multi-temporal satellite imagery and DEMs of Difference (DoD) to map channel migration, bank erosion, and sediment flux following mining disturbance — relevant to Ghana's Birim and Offin river systems where alluvial gold operations are concentrated.

MSc / PhD Direction

Geospatial Intelligence for Mineral Resource Assessment in West Africa

Applying supervised machine-learning classifiers to hyperspectral and multispectral imagery to delineate lithological boundaries and alteration zones — extending the structural and satellite analysis methods developed during fieldwork in the Voltaian Basin.

Environmental Data Infrastructure for Ghana's Extractive Sector

Drawing on my PostgreSQL sample-tracking architecture at the Ghana Geological Survey Authority — scaling data-integrity principles to national-level environmental monitoring networks, including automated QA/QC pipelines and open-access geoportals.

DEM Analysis Alluvial Gold

Applying DEM Analysis to Alluvial Gold Operations in Ghana's Eastern Region

When I joined Hawk Mining Limited, the spatial relationship between the active channel, its floodplain, and subsurface gold-bearing gravels was understood mostly through experience and intuition. My first task was to change that. Using a 12.5m ALOS PALSAR DEM, I delineated the Pra Basin's second-order channels and their associated floodplain extents — producing a metric that we now use directly in weekly resource planning meetings. What I learned about the gap between DEM theory and field reality is worth writing down.
